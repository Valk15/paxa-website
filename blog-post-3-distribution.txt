--- VERSION 1 — Problem-led (for UK Dog Owners) ---

Has anyone else noticed that the worst destruction happens in the first half hour after you leave — and then your dog seems weirdly calm on camera by the time you get home?

There is a reason for that. Cortisol — the stress hormone — follows a predictable curve during separation. It peaks around 30 to 40 minutes after departure. That is when the barking, scratching, and chewing are at their worst. After that, the adrenal system essentially crashes. The dog is not calm. They are exhausted.

Understanding this pattern changed how I approached training. Instead of trying to fix eight hours of anxiety, I realised I only needed to fix 40 minutes.

Wrote more about the science here: https://paxapet.co.uk/blog/the-40-minute-rule-dog-separation-anxiety/


--- VERSION 2 — Question-led (for Dog Separation Anxiety Support) ---

Quick question for anyone dealing with separation anxiety — have you ever checked your camera two hours in and thought "oh, they seem fine now"?

That is not your dog coping. That is the cortisol crash. Research shows separation anxiety peaks in the first 30 to 40 minutes. The panic, the pacing, the vocalisation — it all concentrates in that window. After the peak, the body simply runs out of fuel to sustain the stress response.

This is actually good news for training. It means the problem has a shape — and once you understand the shape, you can work with it instead of against it.

More detail on the science and what to do about it: https://paxapet.co.uk/blog/the-40-minute-rule-dog-separation-anxiety/


--- VERSION 3 — Counter-intuitive hook (for Anxious Dogs UK) ---

Most people assume their dog is anxious the entire time they are gone. The science says otherwise.

Separation anxiety follows a predictable cortisol curve. It surges in the first 30 to 40 minutes — that is when the destructive behaviour happens — then plateaus. Not because the dog has calmed down, but because the adrenal system cannot sustain peak output indefinitely. The quiet dog on the camera at hour three is not relaxed. They are in a state researchers call learned helplessness.

The practical takeaway: effective training targets that 40-minute window specifically, using sub-threshold absences that prevent the cortisol surge from ever starting.

Full breakdown here: https://paxapet.co.uk/blog/the-40-minute-rule-dog-separation-anxiety/
